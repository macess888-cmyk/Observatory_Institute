## 1. Model Purpose

The Minimum Distinguishability Model represents the provisional hypothesis that organized understanding requires relevant differences to remain detectable above a context-dependent minimum threshold.

The model does not claim that:

* distinguishability is universally irreducible;
* identity always precedes relationship;
* stable object boundaries are required in every domain;
* all components must be individually distinguishable;
* distinguishability alone produces understanding.

The model organizes the evidence and open questions produced by ROB-000001 and RR-000001.

---

## 3. Core Proposition

The model proposes:

> Organized understanding remains possible only when relevant differences remain sufficiently detectable for the operations required by the context.

The important term is:

```text
sufficiently distinguishable
```

The model does not require complete, permanent, or absolute separation.

It requires only enough distinguishability to support the relevant operation.

---

## 4. Minimum Distinguishability

Minimum distinguishability is the lowest degree of detectable difference at which a required operation remains possible.

Candidate operations include:

* identification;
* comparison;
* classification;
* relationship formation;
* attribution;
* interpretation;
* review;
* correction;
* preservation;
* reconstruction.

The threshold may vary by operation and context.

Therefore, no single universal threshold is currently assumed.

---

## 5. Basic Model

The provisional model is:

```text
Relevant Difference
        │
        ▼
Detectable Distinction
        │
        ▼
Operationally Identifiable Positions
        │
        ▼
Comparison or Relationship
        │
        ▼
Organized Interpretation
        │
        ▼
Review and Correction
```

This sequence represents one possible dependency direction.

It is not yet established as the only direction.

---

## 6. Threshold Representation

Let:

```text
D = available distinguishability
Tₒ = minimum distinguishability threshold for operation o
```

An operation remains possible when:

```text
D ≥ Tₒ
```

An operation becomes unreliable, ambiguous, or impossible when:

```text
D < Tₒ
```

This representation is conceptual.

No numerical measurement scale has yet been established.

---

## 7. Context Dependence

The threshold is expected to depend on context.

```text
Tₒ = f(operation, context, evidence, observer, time)
```

For example:

* recognition may require only broad pattern difference;
* attribution may require stable source identity;
* correction may require precise target localization;
* historical reconstruction may require temporal ordering;
* relationship analysis may require distinguishable relational positions;
* statistical interpretation may require distinguishable distributions rather than distinguishable individuals.

Therefore:

```text
Minimum Distinguishability ≠ Fixed Universal Precision
```

---

## 8. Levels of Distinguishability

The model identifies several possible levels.

### 8.1 Component Distinguishability

Individual parts remain distinguishable.

Example:

```text
A ≠ B
```

This supports direct comparison between components.

---

### 8.2 Relational Distinguishability

Objects are distinguished by the positions they occupy within a relationship.

Example:

```text
A → B
```

Even when A and B are otherwise similar, their relational roles may remain different.

---

### 8.3 Contextual Distinguishability

An object becomes distinguishable because the context identifies which differences matter.

Example:

```text
Same object
Different context
Different significance
```

---

### 8.4 Temporal Distinguishability

States are distinguished through sequence or change.

Example:

```text
State at t₁ ≠ State at t₂
```

This supports history, comparison, revision, and reconstruction.

---

### 8.5 Pattern Distinguishability

An overall configuration remains distinguishable even when its individual elements are not.

Example:

```text
Indistinguishable components
        │
        ▼
Distinguishable pattern
```

This level may explain recognition, statistical organization, and collective behaviour.

---

### 8.6 Source Distinguishability

Claims or observations remain attributable to distinguishable sources.

This supports:

* provenance;
* accountability;
* evidentiary review;
* disagreement preservation;
* trust assessment.

---

### 8.7 Status Distinguishability

Different epistemic or operational states remain visible.

Examples include:

```text
Observed ≠ Inferred
Plausible ≠ Established
Reviewed ≠ Accepted
Unknown ≠ False
```

This prevents status collapse.

---

## 9. Distinguishability Gradient

The model does not treat distinguishability as purely binary.

A possible gradient is:

```text
CLEARLY DISTINGUISHABLE
        │
        ▼
SUFFICIENTLY DISTINGUISHABLE
        │
        ▼
PARTIALLY DISTINGUISHABLE
        │
        ▼
OPERATIONALLY AMBIGUOUS
        │
        ▼
INDISTINGUISHABLE
```

Different operations may fail at different points on this gradient.

---

## 10. Operational Consequences

When distinguishability remains above the required threshold:

* objects can be located;
* claims can be attributed;
* differences can be compared;
* relationships can be formed;
* evidence can be reviewed;
* errors can be localized;
* changes can be preserved;
* corrections can target the intended object.

When distinguishability falls below the threshold:

* referents become unstable;
* observation and interpretation may collapse;
* provenance may be lost;
* relationships become ambiguous;
* review becomes unreliable;
* correction may target the wrong object;
* versions may become historically indistinguishable;
* uncertainty may lose its identifiable target.

## 11. Relationship to Identity

The model does not determine whether identity is prior to distinguishability.

Three possibilities remain open.

### Identity-First Model

```text
Identity
    │
    ▼
Distinguishability
```

A thing is distinguishable because it already possesses identity.

### Distinguishability-First Model

```text
Detectable Difference
        │
        ▼
Identity Formation
```

Identity emerges because a difference can be detected and preserved.

### Reciprocal Model

```text
Identity ⇄ Distinguishability
```

Identity and distinguishability may reinforce one another.

No dependency direction is currently established.

---

## 12. Relationship to Relationships

Three possibilities also remain open.

### Distinguishability-First

```text
Distinguishable Positions
        │
        ▼
Relationship Formation
```

Relationships require already distinguishable relata.

### Relationship-First

```text
Relationship
        │
        ▼
Differentiated Positions
```

Relational position produces distinguishability.

### Co-Emergent

```text
Distinguishability ⇄ Relationship
```

Each becomes available through the other.

The current evidence supports continued investigation of all three.

## 13. Relationship to Context

Context may determine which differences are relevant.

A difference may exist without mattering to the current inquiry.

Therefore:

```text
Difference ≠ Relevant Distinction
```

A relevant distinction is a difference that affects an operation, interpretation, decision, review, or correction within a defined context.

The model therefore includes:

```text
Difference
    │
    ▼
Contextual Relevance
    │
    ▼
Operational Distinguishability
```

---

## 14. Relationship to Time

Time may preserve distinctions between states even when object identity remains stable.

For example:

```text
Artifact version 0.1
        │
        ▼
Artifact version 0.2
```

The artifact may retain identity while its state changes.

Temporal ordering allows the versions to remain distinguishable without treating them as wholly unrelated objects.

This suggests:

```text
Identity Continuity
        +
Temporal Distinction
        =
Reviewable Change
```

---

## 15. Relationship to Uncertainty

Uncertainty does not eliminate distinguishability.

A research object may remain partly unknown while still being sufficiently identifiable for inquiry.

Therefore:

```text
Unknown ≠ Indistinguishable
```

A valid unknown should retain:

* an identifiable target;
* a bounded question;
* a known evidence limitation;
* a visible status;
* a path for future investigation.

When uncertainty loses its target, it may become unstructured ambiguity rather than preserved unknown knowledge.

---

## 16. Minimum Distinguishability and Correction

Correction requires a target.

A target must be sufficiently distinguishable from:

* neighbouring objects;
* prior states;
* alternative interpretations;
* unrelated failures;
* supporting evidence;
* the correction mechanism itself.

The provisional dependency is:

```text
Distinguishable Target
        │
        ▼
Localized Error or Deficiency
        │
        ▼
Relevant Corrective Action
        │
        ▼
Reviewable Result
```

If the target is not sufficiently distinguishable, correction may become misdirected or non-verifiable.

---

## 17. Minimum Distinguishability and Review

Review requires distinguishable layers such as:

```text
Observation
Interpretation
Assumption
Model
Evidence
Conclusion
```

If these layers collapse, a reviewer may be unable to determine:

* what was directly observed;
* what was inferred;
* which assumptions were introduced;
* which evidence supports which claim;
* where disagreement belongs;
* what should be revised.

The model therefore predicts that review quality declines when epistemic layers become insufficiently distinguishable.

---

## 18. Minimum Distinguishability and Preservation

Preservation requires more than retaining content.

It may also require preserving distinctions between:

* versions;
* sources;
* contexts;
* classifications;
* states;
* interpretations;
* decisions;
* revisions.

Without these distinctions, content may survive while organized understanding does not.

Therefore:

```text
Content Preservation ≠ Structural Preservation
```

and:

```text
Memory Exists ≠ Understanding Is Recoverable
```

---

## 19. Failure Modes

The model identifies several provisional failure modes.

### 19.1 Identity Collapse

Distinct objects become treated as the same object.

### 19.2 Context Collapse

Information from different contexts becomes merged without visible boundaries.

### 19.3 Status Collapse

Observed, inferred, reviewed, accepted, and unknown states become indistinguishable.

### 19.4 Temporal Collapse

Earlier and later states lose ordering or version identity.

### 19.5 Source Collapse

Claims lose attributable origin.

### 19.6 Relationship Collapse

Relational positions become ambiguous or interchangeable.

### 19.7 Scale Collapse

A distinction valid at one level is incorrectly assumed to exist or not exist at another level.

### 19.8 Pattern-Component Confusion

Global pattern distinguishability is mistaken for component distinguishability, or the reverse.

---

## 20. Counterexample Accommodation

The model must accommodate cases in which:

* a pattern is recognized without individual component identification;
* tacit knowledge exists without explicit distinction;
* a collective behaves coherently without stable individual identity;
* ambiguous language still communicates;
* statistical organization exists without persistent object-level identity;
* a whole is perceived before its parts.

These cases may indicate that distinguishability is required at some level, but not necessarily at every level.

The model therefore proposes:

> Organized understanding may require sufficient distinguishability at the level relevant to the operation, rather than complete distinguishability at all levels.

This remains provisional.

---

## 21. Preliminary Model Predictions

The model predicts that:

1. Removing all relevant distinctions will reduce or eliminate organized interpretation.
2. Different operations will fail at different distinguishability thresholds.
3. Correction will require finer distinguishability than broad recognition.
4. Attribution will require source distinguishability.
5. Historical reconstruction will require temporal distinguishability.
6. Pattern understanding may survive component-level indistinguishability.
7. Relationship formation may generate distinctions rather than merely depend on them.
8. Context may restore distinctions that are absent from isolated data.
9. Preserved uncertainty will require an identifiable target.
10. Loss of status distinctions will increase unsupported certainty and review failure.

These predictions require testing.

## 27. Model Revision Conditions

RM-000001 should be revised when evidence demonstrates that:

* distinguishability is unnecessary for a relevant operation;
* relationship fully explains distinguishability;
* identity fully explains distinguishability;
* context alone restores every required distinction;
* distinguishability thresholds can be measured;

* a counterexample invalidates the model’s core proposition;
* an additional candidate structure is required.

Revision must preserve prior versions and record the evidence that required change.

## 28. Model Conclusion

The Minimum Distinguishability Model provides a provisional structure for investigating whether organized understanding depends on detectable, context-relevant difference.

The model proposes that different operations require different minimum levels and forms of distinguishability.

It preserves the possibility that distinguishability is:

* primary;
* derivative;
* relational;
* contextual;
* temporal;
* emergent;
* locally necessary;
* globally unnecessary;
* or irreducible.

The evidence does not yet resolve these possibilities.

The correct status remains:

TEST THROUGH REMOVAL

PRESERVE COMPETING DIRECTIONS
