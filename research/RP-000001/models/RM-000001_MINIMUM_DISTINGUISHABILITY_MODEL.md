## 1. Revision Purpose

Version 0.2 revises the Minimum Distinguishability Model in response to evidence accumulated through:

* ROB-000001 — Distinguishability as a Candidate Structure;
* ROB-000002 — Relational Position Under Weakened Identity;
* ROB-000003 — Pattern Distinguishability Without Component Identity;
* ROB-000004 — Symmetric Positions Without Persistent Labels;
* RR-000001 through RR-000004.

The revision preserves the original proposition that organized understanding requires sufficient detectable difference for relevant operations.

It adds a separate identity-threshold structure and distinguishes among:

* multiplicity;
* pattern identity;
* class identity;
* positional identity;
* unique identity;
* temporary operational identity;
* persistent identity;
* source identity;
* temporal identity.

The revision does not establish distinguishability or identity as irreducible.

## 3. Revised Core Proposition

The revised model proposes:

> Organized understanding requires sufficient distinguishability and sufficient identity for the operation, level, scale, context, and time under investigation.

Distinguishability and identity are related but not identical.

```text
Distinguishability
        ≠
Identity
```

Distinguishability concerns whether a relevant difference can be detected.

Identity concerns whether a target, position, pattern, class, source, or continuing object can be identified sufficiently for a required operation.

---

## 4. Dual Threshold Structure

Let:

```text
D = available distinguishability
I = available identity
Tᴅ(o,l,s,c,t) = distinguishability threshold
Tᵢ(o,l,s,c,t) = identity threshold
```

where:

* `o` = operation;
* `l` = level;
* `s` = scale;
* `c` = context;
* `t` = temporal condition.

An operation is provisionally admissible when:

```text
D ≥ Tᴅ(o,l,s,c,t)
```

and:

```text
I ≥ Tᵢ(o,l,s,c,t)
```

Some operations may require high distinguishability but low persistent identity.

Others may require both high distinguishability and high identity.

---

## 5. Operational Sufficiency

An operation may remain possible when both thresholds are met:

```text
Sufficient Difference
        +
Sufficient Identity
        │
        ▼
Operationally Identifiable Target or Pattern
        │
        ▼
Comparison, Interpretation, Review, or Correction
```

If distinguishability falls below its threshold:

```text
D < Tᴅ
```

relevant differences become ambiguous or unavailable.

If identity falls below its threshold:

```text
I < Tᵢ
```

the observer may detect difference without being able to identify, attribute, track, govern, or correct the relevant target.

## 6. Distinguishability Types

The model now distinguishes at least five forms of distinguishability.

### 6.1 Multiplicity Distinguishability

The observer can determine that more than one occurrence or position exists.

```text
One
        ≠
More Than One
```

Multiplicity does not provide unique identity.

---

### 6.2 Component Distinguishability

Individual components can be separated or compared within the current observation.

This may be temporary and need not support historical continuity.

---

### 6.3 Relational Distinguishability

Positions differ because their relational configurations differ.

```text
Different Relations
        │
        ▼
Different Positions
```

Relational distinguishability weakens under symmetry.

---

### 6.4 Pattern Distinguishability

A global configuration remains distinguishable through:

* topology;
* count;
* arrangement;
* symmetry;
* orientation;
* density;
* sequence;
* collective behaviour.

Pattern distinction may survive component replacement.

---

### 6.5 Status Distinguishability

Epistemic or operational states remain separable.

```text
Observed ≠ Inferred
Plausible ≠ Established
Reviewed ≠ Accepted
Unknown ≠ False
```

Status distinction is required for reviewable research.

---

## 7. Identity Types

The model now distinguishes multiple identity forms.

### 7.1 Token Existence

A separate occurrence is present.

This establishes multiplicity but not persistent identity.

---

### 7.2 Temporary Positional Identity

A position can be referenced during the current observation.

Examples:

```text
left
right
selected
unselected
first
second
```

This identity may disappear when the representation, observer, or operation changes.

---

### 7.3 Relational Identity

A target is identified by a distinctive relational configuration.

Examples:

* source node;
* intermediary;
* final recipient;
* only position connected to an anchor.

Relational identity does not provide uniqueness under perfect symmetry.

---

### 7.4 Class Identity

A member is identifiable as belonging to an equivalence class.

Example:

```text
one of the symmetric positions
```

Class identity permits counting and collective operations but not unique attribution.

---

### 7.5 Pattern Identity

A configuration is identifiable as a whole.

Pattern identity may persist despite complete component replacement when the continuity criterion is structural.

---

### 7.6 Unique Identity

A particular member can be distinguished from all equivalent members.

Unique identity may depend on:

* asymmetric relation;
* intrinsic property;
* persistent label;
* external anchor;
* coordinate;
* temporal history;
* source record;
* observer selection.

---

### 7.7 Persistent Identity

A target can be reidentified across time, state change, movement, or representation.

Persistent identity supports:

* history;
* recurrence detection;
* responsibility;
* provenance;
* continuity;
* targeted correction.

---

### 7.8 Source Identity

An observation, claim, decision, or action remains attributable to an identifiable source.

Source identity supports evidentiary review and accountability.

---

### 7.9 Temporal Identity

A target remains connected across successive states.

```text
Target at t₁
        │
        ▼
Target at t₂
```

Temporal identity may remain uncertain even where present-state distinction is clear.

---

### 7.10 Operational Identity

An operation creates a temporary target through selection, measurement, or intervention.

```text
Equivalent Members
        │
        ▼
Selection
        │
        ▼
Selected Target
```

Operational identity may be sufficient for immediate action but insufficient for audit or historical continuity.

## 9. Level Dependence

Distinguishability and identity may differ by level.

```text
Component Level
Relational Level
Pattern Level
Class Level
Institutional Level
System Level
```

A system may preserve pattern identity while losing component identity.

```text
Pattern Continuity
        ≠
Component Continuity
```

A system may preserve component identity while transforming its global pattern.

```text
Component Continuity
        ≠
Pattern Continuity
```

---

## 10. Scale Dependence

Observed distinguishability depends on scale.

Candidate scales include:

* spatial;
* temporal;
* semantic;
* statistical;
* organizational;
* computational;
* institutional.

A difference visible at one scale may disappear at another.

```text
Observed Distinguishability
        =
Function of Observation Scale
```

A valid model must state the scale at which a distinction or identity claim is made.

---

## 11. Context Dependence

Difference alone is not sufficient.

A difference must be relevant to the current operation or question.

```text
Difference
        ≠
Relevant Distinction
```

Context determines:

* which differences matter;
* which identities must persist;
* what counts as continuity;
* what correction requires;
* what level of explanation is sufficient.

---

## 12. Observer Dependence

An observer may introduce distinction through:

* selection;
* labeling;
* measurement;
* ordering;
* coordinates;
* comparison;
* recording.

Therefore:

```text
Observed Distinction
        ≠
Pre-existing Structural Distinction
```

Observer-created distinction may be operationally valid while remaining representational or temporary.

The model does not determine whether all distinction is observer-dependent.

---

## 13. Representation Dependence

A diagram may create apparent identity through layout.

For example:

```text
left node
right node
```

may not correspond to any distinction in the abstract structure.

The model separates:

```text
Representational Identity
Structural Identity
Operational Identity
Persistent Identity
```

These must not be treated as equivalent without evidence.

---

## 14. Symmetry

Symmetry exists when positions can be exchanged without changing the represented structure.

Under relational symmetry:

```text
? ↔ ?
```

the structure may preserve:

* multiplicity;
* pattern;
* class identity;
* relation type.

It may fail to preserve:

* unique positional identity;
* occupant continuity;
* member-specific attribution;
* member-specific history.

---

## 15. Symmetry Boundary

The revised model constrains the relationship-first proposition.

```text
Relationship
        │
        ▼
Unique Positional Identity
```

is not universally valid.

A more accurate form is:

```text
Non-equivalent Relational Configuration
        │
        ▼
Relational Distinguishability
        │
        ▼
Possible Unique Positional Identity
```

Under perfect symmetry:

```text
Equivalent Relational Configuration
        │
        ▼
Class Identity
        +
Multiplicity
        +
Pattern Identity
        │
        ▼
No Unique Member Identity from Relation Alone
```

---

## 16. Symmetry-Breaking Conditions

Unique identity may emerge through:

* persistent labels;
* intrinsic differences;
* external anchors;
* asymmetric relations;
* temporal events;
* unique histories;
* coordinates;
* source records;
* observer selection;
* unequal roles.

```text
Symmetry
        +
Differentiating Condition
        │
        ▼
Unique Operational Position
```

These conditions solve local differentiation while potentially relocating the identity dependency.

---

## 17. Anchor Regress

An external anchor can distinguish a symmetric member.

```text
Anchor → Member
```

But the anchor must itself remain identifiable.

This creates a possible regress:

```text
What identifies the member?
        │
        ▼
The anchor
        │
        ▼
What identifies the anchor?
```

The model does not resolve whether the regress terminates in:

* intrinsic identity;
* embodied continuity;
* primitive distinction;
* context;
* convention;
* observation;
* another relation.

## 19. Pattern Identity

Pattern identity concerns continuity or recognition of a configuration as a whole.

It may depend on:

* topology;
* function;
* role;
* boundary;
* history;
* interpretation;
* causal mechanism.

Pattern continuity is therefore criterion-dependent.

```text
Same Pattern
```

must specify:

```text
Same in what respect?
```

Structural continuity does not automatically imply material, causal, functional, historical, or semantic continuity.

## 20. Recognition and Understanding

The model now distinguishes:

```text
Difference Detection
        │
        ▼
Pattern Recognition
        │
        ▼
Pattern Classification
        │
        ▼
Structural Interpretation
        │
        ▼
Causal Explanation
        │
        ▼
Semantic Understanding
```

Recognition is not equivalent to complete understanding.

However, recognition and classification may constitute limited organized understanding when they support valid comparison or operation.

## 21. Global and Local Understanding

A system may remain globally understandable while becoming locally opaque.

```text
Global Pattern Visibility
        ≠
Local Explainability
```

Global understanding may preserve:

* trend;
* distribution;
* congestion;
* topology;
* direction;
* collective state.

Local understanding may require:

* component identity;
* source identity;
* event history;
* authority;
* provenance;
* individual state.

## 22. Operation-Specific Thresholds

Different operations require different thresholds.

| Operation                     | Distinguishability Requirement | Identity Requirement             |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------- |
| Count members                 | Multiplicity                   | Token existence                  |
| Recognize a pattern           | Pattern-level                  | Pattern identity                 |
| Compare topology              | Relational or pattern-level    | Class or positional identity     |
| Attribute authorship          | Source-level                   | Persistent source identity       |
| Detect recurrence             | Temporal distinction           | Persistent identity              |
| Assign responsibility         | Actor distinction              | Attributable identity            |
| Localize a failure            | Positional distinction         | Target identity                  |
| Correct any equivalent member | Class distinction              | Temporary operational identity   |
| Correct a specific member     | Unique distinction             | Persistent target identity       |
| Preserve history              | Temporal distinction           | Persistent temporal identity     |
| Govern collectively           | Class distinction              | Role or class identity           |
| Hold an actor accountable     | Unique actor distinction       | Persistent attributable identity |

The table is provisional.

## 23. Correction Thresholds

Correction requires a target.

### Pattern-Level Correction

May target:

* configuration;
* distribution;
* global constraint;
* shared rule;
* system capacity.

This may not require unique component identity.

### Class-Level Correction

May target any interchangeable member.

This requires class identity and temporary target selection.

### Member-Specific Correction

Requires:

* unique target identity;
* continuity;
* localization;
* intervention reachability;
* post-correction verification.

```text
Failure Localization
        ≠
Corrective Target Identity
```

---

## 24. Governance Thresholds

Governance may occur at multiple levels.

### Collective Governance

Applies policy or constraints to a class or system.

### Role Governance

Binds authority to a role.

### Actor Governance

Binds authority, responsibility, and consequence to an identifiable actor.

```text
Role Capacity
        ≠
Actor Accountability
```

Governance involving consequence requires stronger identity than process visibility alone.

---

## 25. Review Thresholds

Review requires distinguishable layers:

```text
Observation
Evidence
Interpretation
Assumption
Model
Review
Conclusion
```

It also requires identity sufficient to trace:

* supporting observations;
* authorship;
* version;
* source;
* reviewed target;
* revision history.

Status collapse or source collapse weakens reviewability.

---

## 26. Preservation Thresholds

Content survival is not sufficient for organized preservation.

```text
Content Preservation
        ≠
Structural Preservation
```

Preservation may require:

* source distinction;
* version distinction;
* context;
* status;
* temporal order;
* relationship history;
* identity continuity.

Without these, information may survive while understanding becomes unrecoverable.

---

## 27. Uncertainty

Unknown conditions remain valid when their target remains sufficiently identifiable.

```text
Unknown
        ≠
Indistinguishable
```

A preserved unknown should contain:

* an identifiable target;
* a bounded question;
* a visible evidence limitation;
* a declared status;
* a possible path for further inquiry.

When the target cannot be identified, uncertainty may collapse into unstructured ambiguity.

---

## 28. Failure Modes

### 28.1 Multiplicity Collapse

Several occurrences are treated as one.

### 28.2 Identity Collapse

Distinct targets are treated as the same target.

### 28.3 Class-Member Collapse

Class-level identity is mistaken for unique member identity.

### 28.4 Pattern-Component Collapse

Pattern identity is mistaken for component continuity.

### 28.5 Status Collapse

Observed, inferred, reviewed, and established states become merged.

### 28.6 Temporal Collapse

Earlier and later states lose order or continuity.

### 28.7 Source Collapse

Claims lose attribution.

### 28.8 Symmetry Concealment

Member exchange or replacement becomes invisible inside a preserved pattern.

### 28.9 Representation Leakage

Layout or labels are mistaken for intrinsic structure.

### 28.10 Scale Collapse

A distinction valid at one scale is generalized to another.

### 28.11 Anchor Failure

A differentiating anchor loses identity or continuity.

### 28.12 Operational Identity Loss

A selected target cannot be reidentified after intervention.

## 29. Revised Model Predictions

The model predicts:

1. Organized understanding can survive without unique member identity for some operations.
2. Multiplicity can remain visible where unique identity is unavailable.
3. Pattern identity may survive complete component replacement.
4. Relationship generates unique identity only where relational asymmetry exists.
5. Symmetry preserves class identity while weakening unique identity.
6. Selection can create temporary operational identity.
7. Temporary identity may support action but fail audit and continuity requirements.
8. Global correction may succeed without local identity when the target mechanism is global.
9. Member-specific correction requires stronger identity than pattern recognition.
10. Governance accountability requires stronger identity than process visibility.
11. Different scales may produce different valid identity descriptions.
12. Anchors solve local differentiation while introducing further identity dependencies.
13. Unknown conditions remain researchable only while their target remains identifiable.
14. Identical patterns may conceal different causes, meanings, risks, and authorities.
15. Model validity will decline where operation, level, scale, context, and time are unspecified.

These predictions require testing.

## 36. Model Conclusion

The revised model proposes that organized understanding depends on two related but distinct requirements:

```text
Sufficient Distinguishability
        +
Sufficient Identity
```

The amount and form required vary by:

```text
Operation
Level
Scale
Context
Time
```

Organized understanding can remain possible where unique identity is unavailable.

However, attribution, continuity, governance, accountability, provenance, and targeted correction require stronger identity than pattern recognition or class-level understanding.

Symmetric relations preserve multiplicity, pattern, and class identity but do not provide unique member identity without an additional differentiating condition.

The current research posture remains:

```text
MODEL PROVISIONALLY
DISTINGUISH DIFFERENCE FROM IDENTITY
PRESERVE LEVELS AND SCALES
TEST TEMPORARY OPERATIONAL IDENTITY
SEARCH FOR COUNTEREXAMPLES
UNKNOWN → HOLD
```
