# Operational Identity Created Through Selection

## 1. Observation Purpose

This observation investigates whether selecting one member of an otherwise symmetric or equivalent class creates sufficient identity for:

* immediate action;
* targeted correction;
* continuity;
* audit;
* review;
* post-action verification.

It tests the distinction between temporary operational identity and persistent identity introduced in RM-000001 Version 0.2.

This observation does not assume that selection creates intrinsic or permanent identity.

---

## 2. Research Prompt

> Does selecting one member of an otherwise symmetric class create sufficient identity for correction, continuity, and review?

The central comparison is:

```text
Equivalent members
        +
Target selection
        =
What identity becomes available?
```

---

## 3. Observation Setup

Consider two structurally equivalent members:

```text
? ↔ ?
```

Neither member possesses:

* a persistent label;
* a unique relational position;
* a recorded history;
* an intrinsic distinguishing property;
* an external identity anchor.

Before selection, both members belong to the same equivalence class.

Now select one member:

```text
[selected] ↔ [unselected]
```

Selection introduces an operational difference.

---

## 4. Initial Observation

Selection creates an immediate distinction between:

* the selected member;
* the unselected member.

The selected member becomes identifiable for the current operation.

This supports:

```text
Selection
        │
        ▼
Temporary Asymmetry
        │
        ▼
Operational Identity
```

The member was not uniquely identifiable through the original symmetric relation.

It becomes uniquely identifiable through the selection event.

---

## 5. Operational Identity

Operational identity is the identity assigned to a target through an active operation such as:

* selection;
* measurement;
* labeling;
* reservation;
* locking;
* intervention;
* inspection;
* correction.

It answers:

> Which member is the current target?

Operational identity may be sufficient even when persistent historical identity is absent.

---

## 6. Immediate Correction Test

Suppose the selected member requires correction.

```text
[selected target]
        │
        ▼
corrective action
```

If the selection remains stable during the action, the system may:

* direct intervention;
* isolate the target;
* modify its state;
* compare before and after;
* distinguish it from non-target members.

This suggests that temporary operational identity can support immediate targeted correction.

---

## 7. Selection Stability Requirement

Immediate correction depends on selection stability.

The selected target must remain distinguishable throughout:

```text
selection
    ↓
intervention
    ↓
verification
```

If the selection marker is lost before completion, the system may no longer know which member was targeted.

Therefore:

```text
Selection Created
        ≠
Selection Preserved
```

and:

```text
Operational Identity Available
        ≠
Operational Identity Stable
```

---

## 8. Target Exchange Test

Consider:

```text
t₀:
[selected A] ↔ [B]
```

Before intervention, the occupants exchange positions:

```text
t₁:
[B] ↔ [selected position]
```

If the selection attaches to the position rather than the occupant, the intervention may affect B instead of A.

If the selection attaches to the occupant, the target may remain A despite movement.

This reveals two different target identities:

### Position-Bound Selection

The selected location remains the target.

### Occupant-Bound Selection

The selected member remains the target.

These are not equivalent.

## 9. Selection Attachment

A valid operational identity must specify what the selection attaches to.

Possible attachment targets include:

* current position;
* physical object;
* logical object;
* role;
* account;
* process;
* event;
* state;
* record;
* source.

Without attachment semantics, the phrase:

```text
selected target
```

may remain ambiguous.

## 10. Label Persistence Test

Suppose the selected member receives a temporary label:

```text
TARGET-1
```

The label supports reidentification only while the association between label and target remains preserved.

Failure conditions include:

* label removal;
* duplicate label assignment;
* label transfer;
* stale reference;
* target replacement;
* record corruption;
* position exchange;
* observer disagreement.

Therefore:

```text
Label Exists
        ≠
Label Remains Correctly Bound
```

---

## 11. Binding Requirement

Selection produces useful identity only when the selection is bound to the intended target.

A provisional sequence is:

```text
Candidate Member
        │
        ▼
Selection
        │
        ▼
Target Binding
        │
        ▼
Operational Identity
        │
        ▼
Intervention
        │
        ▼

```

If target binding fails, operational identity may be false or unstable.

## 12. Immediate Action Versus Historical Continuity

A temporary selection may support immediate action without supporting historical continuity.

For example:

```text
Correct the currently selected replica.
```

may succeed.

Later, the system may be unable to answer:

* which physical replica was corrected;
* whether the same replica failed previously;
* whether it was replaced;
* whether the correction persisted;
* whether responsibility attaches to the same member.

Therefore:

```text
Immediate Targetability
        ≠
Historical Reidentifiability
```

---

## 13. Audit Test

For an action to be auditable, the record must preserve:

* what was selected;
* how it was selected;
* when selection occurred;
* what identity attributes were available;
* what action followed;
* whether the binding persisted;
* what result was observed;
* whether the same target was verified afterward.

Selection alone does not produce auditability.

Audit requires preserved evidence of selection and binding.

---

## 15. Post-Action Verification Test

Suppose the selected member is modified.

Before:

```text
[selected member: state 0]
```

After:

```text
[selected member: state 1]
```

Verification requires evidence that:

* the post-action member is the same selected target;
* the observed change resulted from the intervention;
* no exchange or replacement occurred;
* the result belongs to the intended member.

Without continuity evidence, the system may observe improvement without proving that the selected target was corrected.

Therefore:

```text
Observed Result
        ≠
Verified Target Result
```

A continuity bridge connects the target across stages:

Possible continuity mechanisms include:

This observation does not determine which mechanism is universally sufficient.

## 17. Selection Without Persistent Identity

Some operations may not require long-term continuity.

For example:

```text
Select any available replica and restart it.
```

The operation may succeed if:

* all replicas are functionally interchangeable;
* no member-specific history matters;
* the selected target remains stable during the action;
* the result can be assessed at the class or system level.

This supports:

```text
Temporary Operational Identity
        =
Sufficient for Some Immediate Operations
```

---

## 18. Selection With Accountability

Where consequences attach to a specific actor or object, temporary selection may be insufficient.

Examples include:

* disciplinary action;
* financial authorization;
* legal responsibility;
* authorship attribution;
* safety-critical repair;
* access revocation;
* evidence destruction;
* irreversible intervention.

These operations require stronger identity because the target must remain attributable and reviewable.

Therefore:

```text
Operational Targetability
        ≠
Accountable Identity
```

---

## 19. Institutional Example

Consider two reviewers with identical roles.

One reviewer is selected to assess a submission.

The institution can temporarily distinguish:

```text
assigned reviewer
```

from:

```text
unassigned reviewer
```

This may support task routing.

However, accountability requires preserving:

* reviewer identity;
* assignment time;
* authority;
* conflict status;
* submitted review;
* later changes;
* decision influence.

Selection creates a role-specific operational identity.

It does not alone provide sufficient governance identity.

---

## 20. Distributed-System Example

Consider two equivalent service instances.

A controller selects one instance for restart.

Selection may be represented by:

* network address;
* process identifier;
* container identifier;
* orchestration handle;
* runtime lock.

Immediate restart may succeed.

Audit or recurrence analysis requires preserved identity across:

* restart;
* rescheduling;
* replacement;
* migration;
* address change.

A runtime handle may not provide persistent system identity.

## 21. Medical Analogy Boundary

A clinician may select a visible target for intervention.

Immediate targeting requires localization.

Longitudinal care requires continuity of patient, tissue, diagnosis, procedure, and outcome.

This observation uses the analogy only to distinguish immediate targetability from persistent clinical identity.

No medical conclusion is claimed.

Selection breaks operational symmetry.

Before selection:


After selection:

```text
selected member
        ≠
unselected members
```

The difference arises from the operation rather than the original structure.

This supports:


## 23. Does Selection Create Identity or Only Reference?

Two interpretations remain open.

### Identity-Creation Interpretation

Selection creates a new operational identity:

```text
the selected target
```

### Reference-Creation Interpretation

Selection does not create identity.

It creates a temporary reference to an already existing member.

The current evidence does not establish which interpretation is more fundamental.

Operationally, both may support immediate targeting.

## 26. Rebinding Risk

A label or handle may later refer to a different target.

For example:

```text
TARGET-1 at t₁ = Member A
TARGET-1 at t₂ = Member B
```

If rebinding is not recorded, continuity may be falsely inferred.

This creates:

```text
Reference Continuity
        ≠
Target Continuity
```

---

## 27. Replacement Risk

A selected target may be replaced during or after intervention.

The system may preserve the same:

* position;
* role;
* address;
* name;
* process handle;

while the occupant changes.

Therefore:

```text
Role Continuity
        ≠
Occupant Continuity
```

and:

```text
Address Continuity
        ≠
Entity Continuity
```

---

## 28. Class-Level Verification

When any class member is acceptable, post-action verification may occur at the class level.

Example:

```text
At least one healthy replica is available.
```

This may not require proving which exact member was repaired.

However, it cannot support claims such as:

```text
The selected replica was successfully repaired.
```

unless target continuity is preserved.

---

## 29. Correction Outcome Types

The observation suggests three outcome levels.

### System-Level Outcome

The overall system condition improved.

### Class-Level Outcome

An acceptable member of the target class now satisfies the requirement.

### Member-Level Outcome

The exact selected member was changed and verified.

These outcomes require different identity evidence.

---

## 30. Selection and Authority

Selection does not prove authorization.

A system may identify a target without possessing authority to act upon it.

Therefore:

```text
Target Identified
        ≠
Action Authorized
```

A complete correction process may require:

```text
Target Identity
        +
Authority
        +
Admissible Action
        +
Continuity
        +
Verification
```

---

## 31. Selection and Consent

In human or institutional contexts, selection may create a target without creating permission.

Therefore:

```text
Selected
        ≠
Consented
```

and:

```text
Identifiable
        ≠
Governably Reachable
```

This boundary is preserved without making legal or ethical conclusions.

## 32. Failure Modes

### 32.1 Wrong-Target Selection

The selected reference points to the wrong member.

### 32.2 Selection Loss

The target marker disappears before action completion.

### 32.3 Target Exchange

The selected occupant exchanges position with another member.

### 32.4 Reference Rebinding

The same reference later points to a different member.

### 32.5 Replacement Concealment

The selected member is replaced while role or address remains stable.

### 32.6 Audit Break

Selection or action evidence is not preserved.

### 32.7 Verification Drift

Post-action verification measures the class or system rather than the selected member.

### 32.8 Authority Confusion

Selection is mistaken for authorization.

### 32.9 Scope Drift

The selected target changes meaning during the operation.

### 32.10 Expired Identity

A temporary operational identity is reused after its valid period.

## 34. Candidate Finding

The following candidate finding is admitted for review:

> Selection can create sufficient temporary operational identity for some immediate actions, but correction continuity, auditability, accountability, and member-specific verification require preserved target binding beyond the selection event.

This remains provisional.

## 40. Operational Conclusion

Selection can break symmetry and create a temporary target sufficient for some immediate operations.

Selection alone does not provide persistent identity, audit continuity, accountability, or proof that the same target was corrected and verified.

Operational identity must remain bound to the intended target across:

```text
Selection
    ↓
Action
    ↓
Verification
    ↓
Review
```
